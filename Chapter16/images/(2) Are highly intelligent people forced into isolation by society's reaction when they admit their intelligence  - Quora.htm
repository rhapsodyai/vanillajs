How can you meet others like yourself if you're not even permitted to admit you're like yourself? Could this be the reason so many highly intelligent people are neurotic?

Case in point: Let's watch the hatefulness and attacks on my own intelligence unfold here in the answers. In about five minutes I have already had two people say I'm not really that intelligent if my intelligence causes me problems. It seems as though even admitting to intelligence anonymously is enough to bring down judgment. Is it really such a sin to say you have a particular quality that happens to be advantageous? Would someone who is naturally good at socializing and politicking receive such ire for acknowledging their traits and abilities? Or is intellect the only beneficial inborn trait that it is a crime to admit to?

Shava Nerad, A student of cogsci, psyops, neuromarketing, and political campaigns -- & virtue
Written Apr 24

A while ago I answered a question on Quora from a woman who didn't understand her boyfriend's reaction to her.  She was pretty smart but he was highly or profoundly gifted, and it sounded to me like he was striving to turn her into his Pygmalion project.

Shava Nerad's answer to How do you handle someone who has about 40 IQ points on you?

My answer was basically that her boyfriend wasn't probably trying to be a jerk, but was young and was really wishing to just show her how to meet him at his level, flip some switch so she could be like him, because he loved her and couldn't see why she couldn't see the world as he did, which he thought was beautiful and she could do if she just tried.

Even in a venue like Quora where you'd figure the demographic would not be anti-intellectual, the responses to my answer to her included a mix of incredibly hostile responses, which I'd anticipated.  But I really wanted the young woman to be able to try to relate to her boyfriend.

So many of the people who'd already responded had said he was an abusive jerk, and just leave him.  It broke my heart.

But something amazing happened too.  Several things really.  A very significant number of people responded that what I said was the first time someone described just how they felt, and they didn't feel alone.  Or thanked me for standing up for the boyfriend because they thought they were the only one who would ever see it that way and were afraid to speak out for fear of inevitable retaliation.  Some of the love and gratitude from these posts -- I can hardly express it -- so much more than made up for the pedestrian, everyday hostility of the people who told me I had no emotional intelligence, or I was a sociopath for standing up for the boyfriend, or I couldn't be that smart because only one in X million people are that smart, or whatever.

People are petty and mean, but people are loving and generous and yes, profoundly and highly gifted people are often incredibly isolated.

The Quora editors were so impressed with my piece, that they presented it to Business Insider, one of the highest circulation magazines on the net, for syndication.

http://www.businessinsider.com/a...

I have a fairly stellar CV from a long career.  A project I founded about a decade ago made international headlines today, in fact.

http://fortune.com/2016/04/22/fa...

But I never thought some emo relationship advice post for the love-lorn geek on Quora would land me on Business Insider, lol.

My life has always been stranger than fiction.

Yes, being profoundly gifted is a life sentence, but we also are more tolerant of social isolation.

Country roads, take me home… to my friends: How intelligence, population density, and friendship affect modern happiness.

Our high intelligence allows us to overcome evolutionary programming that compels herd behavior.  And thank God!  Or we'd be even more messed up.  We are capable of sublimating our needs into intellectual pursuits, to some extent.

But I long for more people who could see the world as I see it.  Who could see the ethics of post conventional justice and the mechanics of how to fix things as I do, and be an equal partner or colleague or friend, more often (thank God for the MIT community!).  Someone who could understand me, rather than put up with me.

I love people, I get along with people (by being adept at perfect camouflage) but I am a stranger in a strange land.  I will understand you, but for most of you, pretty much guaranteed, all of you, you will not understand me.  It's not elitist, it's how I think, how I model problems, how I see the world in holisms and gestalts you can't hold in your head at once.  It's ok.  You are a almost certainly a happier person.  You are better at dozens of things.  I am not a better person, but I am essentially an alien mind.

And for most people, if I mention that, or show that, their primate ethology will compel them to attack.

You are now free to demonstrate.

Michael Rios, I counsel exceptionally gifted adults,and present workshops on this topic.
Written Nov 21, 2014
Several people, including Doug, have illustrated very clearly the biggest problem that highly intelligent people face--people like Doug.  People who are not at the very high end of the intelligence spectrum not only don't understand what brilliant people have to go through, they don't even get the tolerance that is given to someone who struggles with the language, has MS, or has social phobia. 
   The challenges and problems of the extremely and profoundly gifted are very real, and have been documented extensively by many researchers including Lewis Terman, Miraca Gross, Linda Silverman, and others.
We learn social skills by practicing them.  Most extremely and profoundly gifted children grow up with no one around them who share their interests or insights.  The other children sense the difference immediately, and, in the vast majority of cases, will ostracize them.  The Lord of the Flies was not really fiction. 
   The result is that brilliant children grow up essentially in isolation.  The effects are very damaging, and very long lasting.
Research has shown that the number one factor in friendship formation and selection is intelligence level.   People tend to select others who are within 5-10 IQ points of themselves for their close friends, and this factor *dominates* virtually every other consideration.  So even for people with a "gifted" IQ of 130, there will be dozens of other kids in a normal school that can meet them intellectually and emotionally.  For the child of IQ 160, there might not be even one other child that is a plausible prospect for a friendship.
Even as an adult, unless a person knows *why* their social contacts are so challenging, they will continue to try to "fix" their social issues, often with limited success.  Except in very narrow contexts, such as *parts* of academia or engineering, the society pushes brilliant people into a closet.
So to answer the original poster's question:  is intellect the only beneficial inborn trait that it is a crime to admit to?  No, not the only one, but the rest are all aggravated felonies.  Lovely company to have to keep!
   Some friends of mine and I are in the process of organizing gatherings for brilliant people so that they can have other people to talk to who will not judge them or reject them because they are smart.  And groups like Mensa aren't really relevant--most Mensans are around IQ 130-140, and there are plenty of other folks out there in that range.  But for people in the 150-200 range, it can feel like they aren't even the same species:  other humans aren't interested in the same things, nor even seem to have similar emotional responses (which turns out to be true as well).
  And for those of you who think that highly intelligent people want to feel superior, you are *completely* missing the boat.  One fourth-grade child I worked with who has an IQ near 200 had never had contact with other children anywhere near his intelligence level, and was angry, depressed, and perpetually in trouble.  We took him to a conference for parents of children who were also profoundly gifted where their kids were in attendance as well.  24 hours into the conference, he runs up to me, beaming, his face glowing, and says "Michael-- It is so wonderful here!  I'm below average!!!" 
That's what the vast majority of brilliant people would love most:  somewhere where they can be below average

Doug Dingus, Enjoys interacting with people
Updated Nov 27, 2014
Meta:  Consider reading the comments in addition to this answer.  I took a strong, deliberate path in this dialog.  Intended.  However, slighting smart people, and or the person asking the question was not intended.

Humility goes a long way in this world.  There is a difference between smart and wise.  Additionally, there are many forms of intelligence and people vary considerably in both their overall capacity and in their presentation.

The wise person realizes they are smart enough and they interact with their peers under that same assumption for them, also realizing we vary, willing to offer help and accept it, grant consideration they themselves would consider due and respond emotionally in ways that give good energy, not detract from it.

People, in general, are ostracized when other people find it more difficult to interact with them than the value of interacting with them would otherwise justify.

There's your answer.  Seek wisdom and you will find your intelligence to be an asset, not some attribute you struggle with.

I'm going to add something:  Hate vs disregard or disapproval.

You are confusing the former with the latter.  Hate is a primal thing, somewhat dangerous because it tends to dilute rational thought down to basic reactions.  Disapproval is simply one of the many modes of feedback other people will give you to provide emotional context.

You also are confusing an attack with something you may not feel good about, and here's a pro tip:  You are in charge of your feelings, nobody else.

Secondly, people feel what they feel.  Sharing those things, good or bad, provides an opportunity to resolve or improve mutual feelings among peers.  Negatives are not attacks, unless the intent is to attack.

When you elevate these things to hate and attacks, you actually cause yourself a lot of negative feedback because your interactions will not be consistent with the actual interaction dynamics present, which should nicely explain this:  (copied from above)

People,  in general, are ostracized when other people find it more difficult to  interact with them than the value of interacting with them would  otherwise justify.

So that is the rational, "why" and a bit of wisdom you can start to use right now to improve your human interactions.

There is an upside!  You are in control of this.  Reconsider your default assumptions about people and you are extremely highly likely to find they return that effort with more genuine, easy to understand and positive interactions.

People generally respond with the level of consideration you grant them.

Being smart isn't enough.  It's not some badge or entitlement. 

It just is an attribute --one of many we all carry through our lives.  Grant people more consideration and you will find them responding in like kind. 

Try assuming they are smart enough and you are very likely to find they are.  You are also likely to find their kind of smart can compliment your own and together you are more than when you are apart.  If you are genuine about this, you will forge deep bonds with others you will come to value very seriously.

We earn the respect of our peers and we earn positive interactions by making sure we contribute to the body of them among people. 

Quite simply: Give and 'ye shall receive.

Chingo Huan Mare, >10 years research, >40 IQ tests taken, Author of a new theory on intelligence
Written Oct 1, 2015
I would like to answer through this perspective...

The battle of the intelligent and the stupid for weirdness
When an intelligent man presents something new, intelligent, complex, and effectively not understood to a stupid one, the latter finds it weird, and looks for ways to protect themselves and ultimately “disarm” the former's intelligence. The mechanisms of how this happens are 3:
  1. Association: People tend to associate whatever they find weird themselves with other things they find weird. For example, they may associate the intelligent person with gods, demons, or craziness.
  2. Closest match: When people cannot say what they find weird, they find the closest weird thing they can and attribute the “weirdness” they perceived to it. For example, the stupid may look for something about the intelligent person's hair, and call that weird.
  3. Generalization: A simple example of generalization in action is when someone gets bitten by a dog once. They might then associate all dogs with biting and pain, and from then on think of dogs as aggressive killer beasts which are after them.
Whichever of the 3 it is, when a more intelligent person talks, if it comes across as weird, the paranoia of the stupid will begin.

What is more interesting about this perceived weirdness is that it works both ways: One who calls another weird is usually just as weird to that other person. So, the stupid person is just as weird from the intelligent person’s point of view as the intelligent person is weird from the stupid person’s point of view.

But there is one big difference: If the intelligent person attacks the intelligence of the stupid, they are attacking their weak points. However, when the stupid person attacks the intelligence of the intelligent person, they are attacking their strong points. And the damage each attack does has a different effect.

If someone tells someone else that their dirty house is so, it is more helpful than damaging, and also has a positive message to it: it means it is unhealthy, and they might get infected by bacteria. But if someone tells them that their already clean house is dirty, especially if they spent hours trying to clean it, it is rather infuriating and damaging.

While this is not the best example of demonstrating the difference in attacking one's strong and weak points, it is perhaps clearer why the stupid do more damage than the intelligent, even when they might attack each other for the same thing. Furthermore, the intelligent usually knows how the above mechanisms work.

So, this is why intelligent people would rather hide their intelligence: they receive more damage, and they want to avoid unnecessary conflict. And eventually this can lead them to social isolation.

Alec Sorensen, MS in Instructional Psychology and Technology.
Written Nov 29, 2014
One thing to point out on the literature on intelligence is that many psychologists don't believe that intelligence is a unitary concept: there are many different types of intelligence.  For example: emotional intelligence, social intelligence, musical intelligence and spatial intelligence in addition to the types the people generally associate with IQ: mathematical/logical intelligence and linguistic intelligence.  Nobody is off the charts on all of these things, in fact, people we typically thing of as geniuses tend to be hyper focused on one area (we usually thing of math or science fields) while letting other areas of their lives slide (like their relationships).

So, it's not a given that if you have one kind of intelligence, you won't have social intelligence, as some people are claiming.  It's okay to be smart in one area and not in another--but it is extremely helpful to realize that given the broad range of intelligences, any given person you're talking to is probably more intelligent than you in some area.  This is a base line understanding that will help you in trying to understand other perspectives, and seeing other perspectives is one of the components of interpersonal intelligence (as defined by Gardner).

If a person keeps drawing the conversation, intentionally or unintentionally, to the subjects that one is knowledgeable, or to the areas in which more is more intelligent than the other conversationalists, it's exclusive.  The other conversationalists will either resent that person, or much more likely in my experience, simply not have anything to say and move on to a conversation that they can contribute to.  However, once an individual understands that others have different forms of intelligence and valuable insights, that individual is much more likely to ask others about their areas of expertise.  This has a couple of effects 1) the other conversationalists actually have something to say and 2) most people feel passionate and proud of their strengths, and talking about them generates a positive feeling that they will then also associate with you.

I'm not claiming to be especially intelligent, or at least not to any point that I've felt people look down on me for it, but most people feel socially awkward, misunderstood, or left out at times.  This is not unique to having an a high IQ.  In fact, I would be most people (if they trusted you and were honest) could give you a list of reasons why others look down on them, which is mostly composed of reasons why they look down on themselves.  And I have found that focusing the conversation on other people's interests and strengths avoids this awkwardness that most people feel and generally leads to better relationships.  It's not particularly easy, because it requires empathy, confidence to hold a conversation without relying on the subjects about which one knows the most, and the humility to forget about your own accomplishments and your own fears for a while.  I'm unsure of how much I succeed, but the attempt to focus on others more in conversation has greatly improved my life.

Also, there are times to showcase your intelligence.  You may find, after listening, that the person you're talking ends up discussing the same sorts of topics you would, in which case you can chip in (it helps to gauge whether they're in the mood for a debate or not before launching in to hearty disagreements).  And other people will occasionally want a short opinion from an expert, and much, much more rarely want extended advice, help, or tutoring.  I'm not saying hide your intelligence, and I'm just advocating trying to discover and appreciate others' forms of intelligence first.

But this is exactly why others express such regret about the efforts they are force to make in order to be accepted.  It is not that when they deploy their broader vocabulary they do so to impress people.  It is because making distinctions between things goes hand in hand with thinking about the world in a clearer way, and processing perceptions and insights at a deeper level.  The difference in communication is not primarily because very bright people use archaic and less well-known words, but one that would be a problem even were they to restrict themselves to a very basic vocabulary - they are trying to express thoughts that simply surpass the comprehension of the vast majority of those around them.  It would be possible to get the ideas across, but one would have to write a few books and give a few lecture courses first to take people at their own pace.  And where is the fun (let alone the social acceptance) in that ?
